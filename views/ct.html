Computational Thinking

Computational thinking is not doing the task. Instead, it is thinking about what kind of task it is, whether we can logically assess what we should or shouldn't focus on and what eliminates risk, failures, dead ends. It involves four equally important processes. One is to break down the task into smaller manageable bits to understand and identify the requirements. It is called decomposition. Two is to filter what we need to focus on at the moment. It is called abstraction. Three is to find out similar patterns within the context of the task. It allows us to predict and use shortcuts. It is called pattern recognition. Four is to find commonalities outside the parameters of the task from a similar one. It means adapting certain practices based on a previous or similar task. It is called generalisation. With these four pillars, we create a flow of steps called an algorithm to give directions and describe the steps to finish the task (learningcentral.cf.ac.uk., n.d.).

We (consumers and creators) use computers (or other gadgets) for everyday needs. We can use these devices to solve problems, but before that, we should understand the problem and how to solve it. That is computational thinking - "It simply means to understand the problem and develop possible solutions to present them in a way that a computer and a human can both understand" (BBC Bitesize, 2022). It is finding what logic, theories and commands are input to the computer to do the task.

"Computational thinking builds on the power and limits of computing processes, whether they are executed by a human or by a machineâ€¦.Computational thinking confronts the riddle of machine intelligence: What can humans do better than computers? and What can computers do better than humans? Most fundamentally it addresses the question: What is computable?"
(Wing, J.M. 2006)

We also apply computational thinking in everyday activities like cooking, commuting, socials, etc. The first thing I thought of about computational thinking was how I research, innovate, buy materials and teach (explain) the craft I do or want to do. We discuss/think, plan, analyse, research and query on our own or with peers. We do most of these processes parallelly and repeatedly until we are satisfied with the results. So, computational thinking is not just a computer science skill but a fundamental skill for all (learningcentral.cf.ac.uk., n.d.).

We think and process without thinking much about it. But when we logically give instructions to the computer, even a minute overlooked detail can make the process fail. That could be something we usually do not think about or pass instructions on. Computational thinking also involves setting aside our intuitive actions and sensory reactions and formulating them effectively to avoid errors.

While writing this assignment, I have decomposed what makes up computational thinking, figured out what point can go where tried not to deviate, reduced duplication and followed the rules of the written English language. I would not have finished this piece if I had skipped even one step in that process. Taking away from here, I will require computational thinking in my learning and career to analyse problems and design solutions. In today's workplaces, problem-solving is a valuable skill for most roles.
