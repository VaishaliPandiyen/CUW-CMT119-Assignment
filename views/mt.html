Reflection on Learning

My first takeaway from the Computational Thinking module is that no matter how much I think I know, there will always be something to learn. This realisation changed my attitude towards listening, thinking and engaging with my peers and the class. It was also the time of my first cultural shift from the Indian university approach, where usually, students are given things on their plate. I felt this was the first step in being prepared for the rest of my study here and in any workplace. I remember thinking, "Oh, I know! I knew that already". As I settled in with my peers, I learnt how to explain things patiently using simpler words. Some may call it settling in, but I think that this process of understanding the changing environment, learning to be humble and open to constructive criticism, and navigating the space and resources applies computational thinking.

In this process, I learnt to be attentive to small details. It impacted how I managed planning and breaking down the components to track my learning and balance my day.

Working with binary numbers reminded me to be patient with numbers. In doing so, I have let myself find a way to work with maths/logic again. I think this is crucial and fundamental to my coursework and my career aspirations.

As I was familiar with HTML and CSS, I tried working on the content and design before starting with coding (and using it instead of deviating to fancier stuff).

That came along with learning it is better not to use something we do not understand well. I tried implementing responsive media queries and designing from mobile to desktop (in that order) for the first time.

This module also challenged my problem-solving abilities, which I had missed when I self-learnt web programming earlier. I realise how important it is to approach the blocks of programming required for my portfolio and the module: fundamentals of programming. While solving the BennieScript exercises, I understood the importance of finding simple and direct ways than sticking to the known ones if they are longer and more complex. In trying and failing to replicate the for-loop when only the while-loop was available in the exercise, I knew my mind was stuck in the javascript land. That made me re-evaluate my requirements and tools available and choose necessity over familiarity. Code-along and youtube videos can leave us wondering, “Oh, how did I do that back then”. We might not take in logic when we follow along or copy ideas.

From being disinterested in maths and computer science at school to wanting to use tech in learning and career, this module was my first institutional learning. I have started to unlearn relying on impulses and learn to be consistent, take breaks and be patient. Whether I will be a journalist or a programmer, a steady and consistent approach is essential to continue learning and growing.
